Obstacle Limitation Surfaces (OLS)

Introduction to Obstacle Limitation Surfaces

Obstacle Limitation Surfaces (OLS) are critical for ensuring the safety of aircraft operations around aerodromes. They define the maximum allowable heights for obstacles in the vicinity of an airport, helping to maintain clear flight paths for aircraft during takeoff and landing. Below are key considerations regarding OLS:

Key Components of OLS

OLS consists of several defined surfaces:

  • Approach Surface: A sloping surface extending from the end of the runway, defining the maximum height of obstacles as aircraft approach for landing.
  • Departure Surface: A sloping surface extending from the end of the runway, defining the maximum height of obstacles as aircraft depart.
  • Transitional Surfaces: Surfaces that connect the approach and departure surfaces to the horizontal surface, allowing for a smooth transition in height limits.
  • Horizontal Surface: A flat surface at a specified height above the aerodrome, defining the maximum height of obstacles within the airport boundary.
Calculating Obstacle Heights

The maximum height of obstacles is determined based on the type of surface and the distance from the runway. Hereâ€™s a simplified calculation for determining the maximum height of an obstacle in the approach surface:

Formula: H = (A + B * D) / 2

Where:

  • H: Maximum height of the obstacle (in feet).
  • A: Height of the runway threshold (in feet).
  • B: Slope ratio (e.g., 1:40 for a 1:40 slope).
  • D: Distance from the runway threshold (in feet).

Example Calculation:

If the runway threshold is at 100 feet, the slope ratio is 1:40, and the distance from the threshold is 2000 feet:

H = (100 + (1/40 * 2000)) / 2 = (100 + 50) / 2 = 75 feet

Therefore, the maximum height of an obstacle at this distance would be 75 feet.

Regulatory Frameworks

  • ICAO Annex 14: The International Civil Aviation Organization (ICAO) provides guidelines for OLS in Annex 14, which outlines the standards for aerodrome design and operations.

  • National Regulations: Many countries have their own regulations that align with ICAO standards, specifying the requirements for OLS and the management of obstacles around aerodromes.

  • Local Zoning Laws: Local governments may implement zoning laws that restrict the height of structures in areas surrounding aerodromes to comply with OLS requirements.
